is contemptible. He accepts the love of Dido and
then abandons her to despair and death. There is
no need to emphasize his crime; Virgil himself
has done that sufficiently. The splendid passage
(4. 305-392) which describes the final interview
between Aeneas and the queen is a masterpiece.
To an appeal which would move a stone Aeneas
replies with the cold and formal rhetoric of an
attorney. Then Dido bursts into an invective
which, for concentrated scorn, nervous force, and
tragic grandeur, is almost unequalled. Finally,
sweeping from the room, she sinks swooning into
the arms of her attendants, while Aeneas is left
stammering and ‘preparing to say many things '—
